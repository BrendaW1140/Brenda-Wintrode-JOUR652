State legislative caucus committees provide a bypass of finance limits

By Brenda Wintrode

Candidates pooled money into state caucus committees.

In the 2018 Maryland state elections, candidates transferred millions of dollars from their campaign accounts to four central pots of money divided by house and party, called legislative party caucus committees.

Created in 2013 by a state campaign reform bill, the caucus committees provide a vehicle that circumvents the $6,000 contribution limit, and as written, provides a potential financial benefit to a candidate of up to $1,979,652.50.

“I bet 99% of voters don’t know about these caucus committees,” said James Appel, treasurer for the Republican caucus committees in both the House and Senate.

Anne Arundel County candidates transferred $1.55 million to caucus committees.

Studying the campaign spending of one county revealed how candidates from both parties used their money. Of the county's total spending 40% was spent on marketing and 35% was transferred to state caucus committees.

Source: Maryland Campaign Information System

Coordinated in-kind contributions

According to the Maryland State Board of Elections “Summary Guide: Maryland Campaign and Finance Laws," caucus committees can transfer up to $24,000 to an individual campaign account, four times as much as a candidate-to-candidate or individual-to-candidate-donation, but Appel said the caucus committees generally spend money on behalf of candidates by making “coordinated in-kind contributions.”

...state and county caucus committee funds are used strategically...

The summary guide provides an example.

“A coordinated campaign contribution could be a television ad campaign that is paid for by the party but that was coordinated with the candidates benefiting from the campaign.”

A coordinated contribution, or an expense on behalf of one candidate, from a state caucus committee can be up to $1 per every two registered voters, according to the summary guide. A worksheet provided by the board of elections for the 2018 election cycle said there were 3,959,305 eligible active voters in Maryland. This means up to $1,979,652.50 is potentially available from a state caucus committee per candidate.

There are county central committees that are governed these guidelines. If the donation comes from a county caucus or central committee, the amount the committee can donate as an in-kind coordinated contribution is $1 for every 2 eligible active voters in that county.

Brooks Schandelmeier, treasurer for the Anne Arundel County Democratic Central Committee, said that it’s typical for the leaders of both parties to raise money for their respective house and senate caucus committees.

“What you do as a leader is you raise a bunch of money, and you give it to the house caucus or you give it directly to other campaigns,” Schandelmeier said, who also serves as president for the Democratic club in District 30.

Schandelmeier said, that for the Democrats, state and county caucus committee funds are used strategically to support vulnerable Democrats or challenge a vulnerable Republican seat.

Campaign Finance Reform Act of 2013

The “Campaign Finance Reform Act of 2013,” introduced by the late Democratic Speaker of the House Michael E. Busch and signed by then-Gov. Martin O’Malley (D), mandated that each house of the General Assembly create its own caucus committee and documented caucus committee campaign donation and spending limits, according to the bill’s fiscal policy note.

"The monies spent by the caucuses are usually guided by the caucus leaders, but others can also play a role," said Appel.

Busch, a popular, decades-long party leader in Annapolis who passed away in April, transferred more than $1 million to the House Democratic Caucus Committee in the 2018 election cycle.

"The monies spent by the caucuses are usually guided by the caucus leaders, but others can also play a role," said Appel.

Six campaigns' expenses

Of Del. Nic Kipke’s $260,140.27 in total spending, he gave $162,800.32 to the Republican House Caucus Committee. The late House Speaker Mike Busch transferred $1,015,000 to the House Democratic Caucus Committee. That's 65% of his total expenditures of $1,552,806.10.

Source: Maryland Campaign Information System

According to Appel, the Republican leaders making these decisions in 2018 were primarily Senators J.B. Jennings and Stephen Hershey and on the House side, Delegates Nic Kipke and Kathy Szeliga.

For the Democrats, in 2018, late Speaker Mike Busch and then Senate President Thomas V. Mike Miller Jr., along with their staff members, would have made these decisions, according to Schandelmeier.

“Donors to individuals are usually ok with their contributions going towards a common goal of their candidate, but that can be a concern,” said Appel. “I don’t recall hearing an individual donor being upset about the transfer.”

The caucus committees spent hundreds of thousands of dollars hiring out-of-state political strategy firms.

“We hire consultants all the time and that’s not considered coordinated expenses that’s just money spent to educate voters or to do an issue or to do polling,” said Appel.

Appel said accountability to donors is key for continued fundraising success.

“You need to demonstrate that you really understand the issues and portray how the money will be spent,” Appel said. “(Candidates) need to be able to thoroughly explain how their money is going to be used and how a contribution to that entity will benefit (donors) in the long run.”

Caucus contribution limits

The contribution limit for an individual to a caucus when the money will be used for campaign purposes is $6,000. However, unlimited funds can be donated by an individual to a caucus committee as long as the donation is earmarked for administrative use, according to the summary guide.

“Donations to an administrative account are not subject to the contribution limits, but disbursements from an administrative account may be made only for non-electoral purposes,” according to the bill’s policy note.

By Maryland law, one candidate can donate to another candidate’s campaign up to $6,000 but can donate an unlimited amount to a caucus committee.

Caucus committees can surpass the $6,000 donation limit up to $24,000 per individual campaign.

For all of the money sitting in the caucus committees, little of it went back to candidates’ accounts. In Anne Arundel County, for example, candidates of both parties collectively donated $1.55 million to caucus committees but only $38,000 came back to campaign accounts.

An analysis of Anne Arundel County's campaign spending.

Appel could not imagine a political campaign being run without money.

“There’s no way to get your word out if you can’t spend money on advertising,” said Appel, who also serves as treasurer on a number of campaigns for both party’s candidates.

Anne Arundel County candidates spent $1,791,889.60 on marketing.

Candidates from both parties spent the majority of their marketing budgets on printing and direct mailing.

Source: Maryland Campaign Information System

An analysis of the campaign finance reports of Anne Arundel County provides an example of spending ratios.

...the top spenders won their races.

County candidates spent over $4.4 million on their campaigns, transferred $1.55 million to caucus committees and spent $1.7 million of their own campaign budgets on direct marketing, media and postage to target voters.

On average, the county’s candidates spent 59% of their budgets on marketing. Thirty-three percent of the candidates spent 75% or more of their total expenditures on marketing.

County candidates who spent the most won.

Anne Arundel County candidates spent $4.4 million on their 2018 campaigns. Those who spend the most won. Those who spend the least lost.
