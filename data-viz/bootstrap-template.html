One county's candidates contributed $1.55 million to legislative party caucus committees

By Brenda Wintrode

Candidates pooled money into state caucus committees.

In the 2018 Maryland General Assembly elections, Anne Arundel County candidates entrenched in districts with loyal party followings donated extra campaign funds - $1.55 million - into one of four central pots of money by house and party, called state legislative party caucus committees.

A review of campaign finance records showed that, of the money donated to state caucuses, only $38,000 made its way back to any Anne Arundel County state legislator's campaign. Both parties spent hundreds of thosands of dollars hiring out-of-state political strategy and marketing consultants.

Anne Arundel state candidates spent $4,477,440.86 running for office in 2018.

35% went to state caucus committees.

Source: Maryland Campaign Information System

Chairman of the Anne Arundel Republican Central Committee James Appel could not imagine a political campaign being run without money.

“There’s no way to get your word out if you can’t spend money on advertising,” said Appel, who also serves as treasurer on a number of campaigns for both party’s candidates.

Both party's candidates spent $1,791,889.60 on marketing.

Source: Maryland Campaign Information System

Campaign finance reports revealed the county's state candidates also spent over $1.7 million of their own campaign budgets on direct marketing, media and postage to target voters.

On average, candidates spent 59% of their budgets on marketing. Thirty-three percent of the candidates spent 75% or more of their total expenditures on marketing.

Top spenders overwhelmingly won their campaigns.

Regardless of how candidates spent their money, the top spenders won their campaigns. Seventy-five percent of the top 20 spenders won, and only 25% of the bottom 20 spenders won.

90% of the top 10 spenders won.

Caucus committee donors can give more than the $6,000 per-election-cycle limit.

Candidates, citizens and other entities can contribute to state caucus committees with names such as the Senate Republican Caucus Committee, the Democratic Senate Caucus Committee or the like on the House side, just as they would a candidate. Except, they can give more because the campaign finance limits for caucus committees are different.

Donors to a party caucus committee can give more than the $6,000 per-election-cycle limit, and the caucuses are then able to donate more than $6,000 to candidates of their choosing.

According to the Maryland Board of Elections, “Maryland Candidacy & Campaign Finance Laws Summary Guide,” a candidate can donate unlimited funds to a caucus committee, and so can a citizen, as long as the citizen earmarks the donation for administrative use.

Caucuses can donate $24,000 to one candidate or up to 50 cents per registered voter in the state as a coordinated campaign contribution.

$1,979,652.50:
The amount available to a candidate from a state caucus committee for a coordinated campaign donation, according to the Maryland Board of Elections.

The summary guide defines a coordinated campaign contribution as “an expenditure made by the party central committee or legislative party caucus committee for the purpose of supporting one or more candidates and done with direct approval, knowledge, and cooperation of the candidate(s)."

According to the Maryland State Board of Elections in-kind contributions worksheet used for the 2018 election cycle, there were 3,959,305 eligible active voters in Maryland, which means up to $1,979,652.50 is potentially available from a state caucus committee per candidate.

"The monies spent by the caucuses are usually guided by the caucus leaders, but others can also play a role," said Appel.

According to Appel, the Republican leaders making these decisions in 2018 where primarily Senators J.B. Jennings and Stephen Hershey and on the House side, Delegates Nic Kipke and Kathy Szeliga.

For the Democrats, in 2018, late Speaker Mike Busch and then Senate President Thomas V. Mike Miller Jr., along with their staff members would have made this decision, according to Brooks Schandelmeier, treasurer for the Anne Arundel Democratic Central Committee.

“Donors to individuals are usually ok with their contributions going towards a common goal of their candidate, but that can be a concern,” said Appel. “I don’t recall hearing an individual donor being upset about the transfer.”

Schandelmeier said that it’s typical for the leaders of both parties to raise money for their respective house and senate committees.

“What you do as a leader is you raise a bunch of money, and you give it to the house caucus or you give it directly to other campaigns,” Schandelmeier said, who serves as president for the Democratic club in District 30.

Speaker of the House Michael E. Busch, a popular, decades-long party leader in Annapolis who passed away in April, transferred more than $1 million to the House Democratic Caucus Committee, slates or other candidates’ campaigns in the 2018 election cycle.

Schandelmeier said, that for the Democrats, these funds are used strategically to support vulnerable Democrats or challenge a vulnerable Republican seat.

“An incumbent can raise more money..."

“Certainly an incumbent can raise money more than a candidate because they’re already there. And the people that are trying to influence those elected officials will give more money,” said Appel.

Del. Nic Kipke (R-Pasadena), incumbent house minority leader, and returning candidate Ron George, this time for a District 30 Senate seat, were the party’s two largest donors to Republican caucus committees, according to campaign finance reports.

Of Kipke’s $260,140.27 in total spending, he gave $162,800.32 to the Republican House Caucus Committee and Annapolis entrepreneur, George, gave $103,690 to the Senate Republican Caucus Committee.

George was the third highest campaign spender and the only candidate of the top ten spenders to lose his election. Democrat Sarah Elfreth won.

Appel said accountability to donors is key for continued fundraising success.

“You need to demonstrate that you really understand the issues and portray how the money will be spent,” Appel said. “(Candidates) need to be able to thoroughly explain how their money is going to be used and how a contribution to that entity will benefit (donors) in the long run.”
