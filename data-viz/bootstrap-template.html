One county’s state candidates spent millions to maintain status quo

By Brenda Wintrode

Over $4.4 million spent

In the 2018 state elections, 40 Anne Arundel County candidates spent over $4.4 million dollars in an effort to win 20 Maryland General Assembly seats, and all of the seats but one remained in the hands of the same party, according to an analysis of their campaign finance reports.

In Districts 21, 31, 32 and 33 both parties spent on average just over $500,000 on their campaigns on marketing, donating to other campaigns and fundraising expenses. District 30 Democrats and Republicans spent over $2.3 million.

Democratic candidates in Districts 30, 30A and 30B spent $591,539.16 marketing their campaigns and a total of $2,049,304.63

District 30 had the highest spending number. Here's why.

The spending anomaly was created by the budget of Speaker of the House Michael E. Busch, a popular, decades-long party leader in Annapolis who passed away in April. During the 2018 election, Busch transferred close to $1 million to the House Democratic Caucus Committee, slates or other candidates’ campaigns.

Brooks Schandelmeier, treasurer for the Anne Arundel Democratic Central Committee, said that it’s typical for the leaders of both parties to raise money for their respective house and senate committees.

“What you do as a leader is you raise a bunch of money, and you give it to the house caucus or you give it directly to other campaigns,” Schandelmeier said, who is also the president of the democratic club in Busch’s former district.

"The money spent by the caucuses are usually guided by the caucus leaders, but others can also play a role," said James Appel, chairman of the Anne Arundel Republican Central Committee and treasurer for several political campaigns. "Donors to individuals are usually ok with their contributions going towards a common goal of their candidate but that can be a concern. I don’t recall hearing an individual donor being upset about the transfer.

Schandelmeier said, that for the Democrats, these funds are used strategically to support vulnerable Democrats or challenge a vulnerable Republican seat.

"An incumbent can raise more money..."

“Certainly an incumbent can raise money more than a candidate because they’re already there. And the people that are trying to influence those elected officials will give more money,” said Appel.

Del. Nic Kipke (R-Pasadena), incumbent house minority leader, and returning candidate Ron George, this time for a District 30 Senate seat were the party’s two largest donors to Republican caucus committees, according to campaign finance reports.

Of Kipke’s $260,140.27 in total spending, he gave $125,000 to the Republican House Caucus Committee and George gave $103,690 to the Senate Republican Caucus Committee.

The largest donation to a Republican Caucus Committee was $125,000 made by Pasadena Republican Del. Nic Kipke.

Candidates received money from donors, from their fellow candidates from other districts and sometimes from their own bank accounts. But once they made that money, how did they spend it? Does more money earned - and ultimately spent - equal a seat in the legislature?

75% of the top 20 spenders won their election. Only 25% of those 20 candidates who spent the least were elected.

Throughout the months leading up to an election, candidates host bonfire parties, pig roasts and pancake breakfasts to earn campaign dollars and votes. Appel said accountability to donors is key for continued fundraising success.

“You need to demonstrate that you really understand the issues and portray how the money will be spent,” Appel said. “(Candidates) need to be able to thoroughly explain how their money is going to be used and how a contribution to that entity will benefit them (donors) in the long run.”

On average, candidates spent 59% of their budgets on marketing. Thirty-three percent of the candidates spent 75% or more of their total expenditures on marketing.

Both party's candidates spent $1,791,889.60 on marketing. What did they use it for?

Source: Maryland Campaign Information System

The amount a candidate needs to spend on marketing is based on how many voters they are trying to reach.

“Door knocking is - dollar for dollar - the most effective way to do it,” Schandelmeier said. “But because districts like here in Anne Arundel County are so spread out, you have to supplement that with mail.”

Schandelmeier added that while knocking on doors costs nothing, the literature candidates hand out during the exchange does not. Candidates should plan to send 3-4 mailers to each targeted voter, and depending on the size of your district, this can get expensive.

Appel pondered the question of what a campaign would be like without money.

“I think it would come down to door knocking,” Appel said. “There’s no way to get your word out if you can’t spend money on advertising.”

“This last election was all about voter turnout,” said Appel. “There were 20,000 more Democrats that came out to vote in the 2018 general election.”

According to the Maryland Board of Elections, 60.1% of eligible voters came out to vote in 2018 as opposed to 49.15% of voters during the last gubernatorial general election in 2014.

For all of the money spent and despite the increase in voter turnout, only one seat changed hands. District 33’s Del. Heather Bagnall (D-Arnold), beat incumbent Republican Tony McConkey by a few hundred votes, turning the district purple for the first time since 1994.
